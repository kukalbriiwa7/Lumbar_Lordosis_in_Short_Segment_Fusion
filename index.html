8 Key Findings Pre-computed from all 761 responses · suitable for abstract / executive summary
1
Sagittal balance is considered clinically relevant by the vast majority of spine surgeons
82% of respondents who completed this section use sagittal balance parameters in pre-operative planning for short-segment fusion. Only 12% agreed that sagittal balance is "not relevant" — with 73% actively disagreeing with that proposition. This definitively counters any suggestion that the topic lacks clinical buy-in.
2
Confidence is the single strongest predictor of parameter adoption
There is a steep confidence–adoption gradient: 98% of "Very Confident" surgeons use sagittal parameters vs 86% of "Confident", 49% of "Low", and only 27% of "Very Low" confidence respondents. Similarly, PI-LL usage drops from 85% → 67% → 33% across confidence tiers. This is the strongest association in the dataset and directly motivates guideline development.
3
Absence of easy-to-use guidelines is the dominant barrier — not perceived irrelevance
45% agree or strongly agree that absence of guidelines is a barrier — the highest of any barrier tested. Importantly, complexity alone is not the issue (only 22% cite "too complex"). The barrier is practical: surgeons believe in the concept but lack accessible tools. MENA respondents show the highest guideline gap acknowledgment at 57%, followed by Latin America (51%) and Europe (47%).
4
North America leads in confidence; Asia Pacific shows largest capacity gap
Regional confidence diverges sharply: North America: 56% "Very Confident" vs Asia Pacific: 18% and MENA: 16%. Whole-spine imaging access in regions without infrastructure further compounds this — surgeons without access use whole-spine imaging 8% vs 51% with access. North America shows 90% sagittal param adoption vs MENA at 65%.
5
PI-LL is the dominant parameter; Roussouly classification is underutilised globally
Among those using parameters, PI-LL / pelvic + regional LL is used always/often by 55%, far ahead of Global SVA/T1PA (40%) and Roussouly (24%). Roussouly uptake varies strikingly by region: Latin America leads at 48% vs North America (25%), suggesting different training traditions. Thoracic parameters remain niche at 10% always/often.
6
Segmental lordosis is frequently planned but difficult to achieve
62% of respondents calculate a target segmental lordosis always or often. However, 17% report often/always difficulty achieving it intraoperatively — with "Confident" surgeons (19%) reporting more difficulty than "Very Confident" ones (13%), suggesting a real surgical challenge rather than a knowledge gap. This supports the case for approach-selection guidance.
7
There is overwhelming demand for an AO smartphone app with measurement capability
84% support an app with both information AND angle-measurement capability — the highest endorsement of any delivery format, ahead of journal publication (69%), video lectures (65%), and info-only app (77%). This preference is consistent across experience levels: trainees (89%), senior surgeons >10yrs (84%), and 0–10yrs (84%). Journal preference is identical across experience groups at ~69%.
8
Lordosis calculation guidance receives near-universal endorsement as an AO priority
When asked about specific AO guidance topics, 86% agree or strongly agree that "how to calculate desirable segmental lordosis" warrants AO-promoted content — the highest value score across all five topics. Approach factors (80%), global imaging timing (80%), and clinical assessment (79%) all receive strong support. There is no topic where support drops below 78%.
